01 Activity

CTA

Using CTA as a basis for the activity design helps us break down complex skills, such as constructive feedback and active listening, and can help to avoid the issue of "expert blindspots." With the CTA model illustrating important decision points and their criteria, it could inform the design of the scenario branches, embedded questions and feedback in a way that helps students master the skill.

Constructive Feedback

Introduction part consists of time, goal, and utility value info for students. Equipped with a big picture (goal), students will be more mentally prepared for the following activity and the time it consumes.

Following learning sciences’ contiguity principle, feedback appears on the same screen as the question. Successful learners, through eye-tracking, were observed to read a portion of text, search a diagram for the object being described, then read a portion of text, search a diagram related to that text, and so forth. (Hegarty, Carpenter, and Just, 1996).

Other than objective questions, we provide learners chances of practicing and explaining. Research has shown that students perform better in Predict-Explain-Observe-Explain (PEOE) condition (Carvalho, 2018) in comparison with answering practice questions.

Active Listening

The model emphasizes that there are three main phases of active listening. The first phase is about preparing your mind for active listening. This involves controlling your thoughts when you are passionate about a particular topic of conversation and also reinforcing that the goal of active listening is to participate in the discussion. The second phase is the phase when you are actually doing the active listening. One can be successful in active listening if they follow certain do’s and don'ts. The do’s include listening with an intention to participate, identifying topic words and sentences to organise information in memory, controlling the urge to respond immediately and keeping your own thoughts on the topic passive. The don’ts include listening with an intention to respond and comparing your thoughts with those of the speaker. Active listening is a metacognitive skill and thus needs some retrospection after every attempt of mastering this skill. So, the third phase is a retrospection phase during which the learner must try to introspect and find what worked well and what did not work so well in terms of the do’s and don'ts. Based on this, the learner must also plan how they will overcome their weaknesses of this attempt in the next attempt of the activity.
